What is the LDS? The Pearson Edexcel Large Data Set contains daily weather observations from 5 UK and 3 overseas Met Office stations. You need to be familiar with its structure, variables, and quirks before your exam — questions will reference it directly.

Key Facts to Know
What years does the LDS cover?
May–October 1987 and May–October 2015
📌 Only May–October — not a full year!
How many stations? Which type?
8 stations total: 5 UK (Camborne, Heathrow, Hurn, Leeming, Leuchars) + 3 overseas (Beijing, Jacksonville, Perth)
What does "tr" mean in rainfall?
Trace — rainfall too small to measure accurately. Treat as 0 in calculations unless told otherwise.
📌 Don't ignore it!
What does "n/a" mean?
Data not available. Exclude these values from any mean/SD calculation — not the same as zero.
📌 Classic exam trap
Which variable is NOT in 1987?
Sunshine hours — only recorded in the 2015 dataset.
📌 Can't compare sunshine across years!
Which variables are UK-only?
Cloud cover (oktas), relative humidity (%), visibility (dam), sunshine hours, max gust, wind direction
What is an "okta"?
Unit for cloud cover. Scale 0–8. 0 = clear sky, 8 = completely overcast (8/8 covered).
What unit is visibility in?
Decametres (dam) — 1 dam = 10 metres. So 1000 dam = 10 km visibility.
📌 Not metres or km!
Which UK station is furthest north?
Leuchars (Scotland, 56.38°N) — consistently the coldest and cloudiest UK station.
Why is Perth unusual?
Southern hemisphere — its seasons are reversed. May–Oct = Australian autumn/winter, so temperatures FALL then RISE (opposite to UK).
📌 Great exam discussion point
What is PMCC used for here?
Testing correlation between two variables, e.g. cloud cover vs sunshine, or temperature vs rainfall. Use the t-test for significance.
Hypothesis test structure?
State H₀ (e.g. μ = 3mm), H₁ (μ > 3mm), significance level, test statistic, compare to critical value, conclude in context.
Common Exam Question Types
1. Summary statistics — Calculate mean, median, variance, SD, IQR from a sample of LDS data. Watch for "tr" and "n/a" values.
2. Hypothesis testing — Test a claim about a population mean using the LDS sample. E.g. "Test at 5% whether mean daily sunshine in Leeming 2015 exceeds 4 hours."
3. Correlation / regression — PMCC between two weather variables. Scatter diagrams, regression lines, interpretation in context.
4. Sampling — Stratified, systematic, or random samples from the LDS. Calculate sample sizes proportional to months or stations.
5. Cleaning data — Identify and deal with "tr", "n/a", outliers. Explain what to do with missing data before calculating statistics.
